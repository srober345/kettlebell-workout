How Body Recomposition Works

You don't need to bulk and cut — science shows you can do both simultaneously

1

Eat at Maintenance

Body recomposition works by eating at or near maintenance calories — not in a large deficit. You fuel muscle growth on training days and tap fat stores on rest days. (PMC, 2024)

2

Prioritize Protein

High protein intake (1.6–2.2 g/kg/day) is the single most important dietary factor. Protein drives muscle protein synthesis and protects lean mass during any deficit. (ISSN Position Stand)

3

Cycle Your Carbs

Eat more carbs on training days to fuel performance and recovery. Reduce carbs on rest days to encourage fat oxidation. Protein and fat stay consistent. (Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024)

4

Train Hard, Eat Smart

Your 6-day program (3 kettlebell + 3 abs) provides the resistance training stimulus. Nutrition supplies the raw materials. Neither works alone for recomposition.

Protein: The Key Nutrient

For adults over 40, protein is the single most protective factor against muscle loss

1

Why 2.0 g/kg

The ISSN and ACSM recommend 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day for exercising individuals. A 2024 RCT showed 1.6 g/kg is superior to 0.8 g/kg for muscle mass gains in older adults. At 48, the higher end of the range (2.0 g/kg = 140g) counteracts age-related anabolic resistance.

2

The Leucine Threshold

Older adults need ~2.8–3.0g of leucine per meal to trigger muscle protein synthesis (MPS). This translates to 30–40g of high-quality protein per meal. Below this threshold, the MPS signal doesn't fire. (Zaromskyte et al., 2021; PMC 2023)

3

Distribute Evenly

MPS was 25% higher when protein was distributed evenly across meals vs. skewed toward dinner (Mamerow et al., 2014). Target ~35g per meal across 4 eating occasions to stay above the leucine threshold each time.

4

Anabolic Resistance

After 40, your muscles become less responsive to protein — a phenomenon called "anabolic resistance." Higher per-meal protein doses and resistance training together overcome this. Your kettlebell program is the resistance stimulus; protein is the raw material.

Meal Timing & Distribution

When you eat matters less than what you eat — but smart timing helps

The "Anabolic Window" Is a Myth

The idea that you must eat protein within 30 minutes post-workout has been debunked. A meta-analysis of 65 RCTs (2,900 participants) found that total daily protein intake — not timing — predicted muscle growth. The anabolic response to training actually persists for 48–72 hours. (Schoenfeld et al., 2013; JOSPT, 2019)

What Does Matter

Distribute protein across 3–4 meals to stay above the leucine threshold each time. MPS peaks within 30 minutes of eating protein and stays elevated for ~3 hours. Spacing meals 3–5 hours apart gives each dose its full anabolic window. (PMC, 2014)

Carbs & Fat: The Supporting Cast

Protein gets top billing, but carbs and fat play critical roles

C

Carbohydrates (280g training / 200g rest)

Carbs are protein-sparing — they prevent your body from burning protein for fuel, leaving it available for muscle repair. They also replenish glycogen depleted during your 1,200-rep kettlebell sessions. Focus on whole grains, rice, potatoes, oats, fruit, and legumes. Avoid ultra-processed carbs (white bread, pastries, sugary cereals).

F

Dietary Fat (90g training / 97g rest)

Fat supports testosterone production — critical for muscle maintenance after 40. It also enables absorption of vitamins A, D, E, and K. Prioritize unsaturated fats: olive oil, avocado, nuts, fatty fish. Limit saturated fat to <10% of calories (~25g). Avoid trans fats entirely.

Hydration

Dehydration reduces strength performance and blunts muscle protein synthesis

Why It Matters for Muscle

When muscle cells lose water, the rate of muscle protein synthesis slows — reducing recovery and growth. Dehydration also increases cortisol, which competes with testosterone receptors, further impairing the anabolic response to training. Studies show dehydrated athletes have reduced multi-rep resistance exercise performance and perceive exercise as harder. (NSCA; Judelson et al., 2007)

How Much

  • Daily baseline: ~100 oz (3L) — active adult male guideline
  • Pre-training: 16–24 oz in the 2 hours before exercise
  • During training: 6–12 oz every 15–20 minutes
  • Post-training: 16–24 oz for every pound of sweat lost

Supplements: Only What Works

Most supplements are marketing. These three have actual peer-reviewed evidence.

Creatine Monohydrate Strong Evidence

The most studied supplement in sports nutrition. Creatine enhances resistance exercise performance by fueling the phosphocreatine energy system, which increases training volume and quality. Meta-analyses show it increases muscle thickness and cross-sectional area in interventions lasting 8–12+ weeks when combined with resistance training. Safe for healthy adults with no evidence of kidney damage. (ISSN; Candow et al., 2022)

Dose: 3–5g per day, every day (no loading phase necessary)

Vitamin D Conditional

Important for muscle and bone function, especially if your serum level is below 30 ng/mL. When combined with resistance training, vitamin D supplementation improves muscle strength and physical function in deficient individuals beyond exercise alone. However, once levels are sufficient (>50 nmol/L), additional supplementation provides no further muscle benefit. Get your levels tested before supplementing. (PMC, 2018)

Dose: 800–2,000 IU/day (if deficient; get blood test first)

Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA) Moderate Evidence

EPA and DHA are components of muscle membrane phospholipids and enhance protein kinase activity and mitochondrial function. They reduce inflammation that can impair recovery. Evidence is strongest in older or nutritionally compromised populations. They act as facilitators of training adaptation rather than direct drivers of muscle growth. Aim to get omega-3s from food (fatty fish 2–3x/week) first; supplement if dietary intake is low. (PMC, 2018; 2020)

Dose: 1–3g combined EPA+DHA per day (from fish oil or algae)

What About Whey Protein?

Whey isn't a "supplement" in the traditional sense — it's just food (a dairy byproduct). It's a convenient way to hit your 140g protein target when whole food meals fall short. Research shows 20–40g of whey protein stimulates MPS in older adults. Casein protein before bed stimulates overnight protein synthesis in both young and elderly men. Use these as tools, not requirements — whole food protein is equally effective.

Common Myths to Ignore

Internet fitness culture is full of bad science. Here's what the research actually shows.

"You must eat within 30 minutes of training or you lose your gains"

The "anabolic window" has been debunked. A meta-analysis of 65 RCTs found timing had no effect on muscle growth. The enhanced anabolic state from training lasts 48–72 hours. Total daily protein is what matters. (Schoenfeld et al., 2013)

"High protein diets damage your kidneys"

For healthy adults, extensive evidence shows protein intakes of 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day are safe with no kidney damage. This myth confuses managing existing kidney disease with causing it. (ISSN Position Stand)

"Your body can only absorb 30g of protein per meal"

Your body absorbs virtually all protein you eat. The 30g number refers to the amount that maximally stimulates MPS per meal in young adults. Older adults may need 40g+ per meal. Protein beyond the MPS cap is still used for other body functions, not wasted. (Schoenfeld & Aragon, 2018)

"You need to eat 6 small meals a day to keep your metabolism high"

Meal frequency has no meaningful effect on metabolic rate or fat loss when total calories are equal. 3–4 meals per day is optimal for protein distribution, but there's no metabolic advantage to eating more frequently. (ISSN Nutrient Timing Position Stand)

"Carbs make you fat"

Calories make you fat — not carbs. Carbohydrates are protein-sparing molecules that augment the muscle-building effects of a high-protein diet. Cutting carbs severely impairs training performance and recovery. (Frontiers in Nutrition, 2024)
